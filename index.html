About this project
Who’s at risk of being miscounted in the 2020 Census?
Month DD, 2019

The decennial census, which aims to count every US resident every 10 years, is critical to a democratic society. It affects federal funding allocations, congressional redistricting, state and local budgets, and data-driven business and research decisions.

But the 2020 Census faces unprecedented threats to its accuracy. Demographic and structural changes over the last decade will make the population harder to count in 2020. And additional factors of under funding, operational changes that remain undertested, and the last-minute introduction of a citizenship question to the 2020 Census could result inan even greater miscount.

To understand how these factors could affect the 2020 Census counts among states and demographic groups, we created projections for three scenarios—designating the miscount risk as low, medium, or high, depending on the factors at play.

We find the national undercount could reach 1.31 percent in the high-risk scenario. But we see a more complicated story at the state and demographic level, as some states (such as California and Texas) and groups (such as people who are black or Latinx, and children younger than 5) face a much greater undercount risk in the 2020 Census. These discrepancies imperil the accuracy and fairness of the census, and they risk diminishing underrepresented communities’ rightful political voice and share of funding.

In the low-risk scenario, we assume there are no other changes to the census process beyond the effect of demographic change in the last decade. We assume US age patterns, race and ethnic distribution, and the proportion of renters will all have changed by 2020, but the accuracy for each group is exactly what it was in the 2010 Census. The 2010 Census was considered one of the most accurate in history.

In the medium-risk scenario, we assume that, in addition to demographic changes, the Census Bureau is accurate in its prediction that the internet self-response mode will produce most of the initial self-responses from households. We also assume that non-response follow up cases are resolved following the 2020 Operational Plan, and that all operations will happen as the Census Bureau expects.

In the high-risk scenario, we use the baseline assumptions about 2020 Census operations from the medium risk scenario (demographic change and operational changes), with one important exception. We assume that self-response rates will be lower than the 60.5 percent rate the Census Bureau predicts. Instead, we use the Census Bureau’s lowest stated value for its projected range of self-response: 55.5 percent. This means that the remaining 44.5 percent of addresses would be handled through non-response follow up. We also assume that response rates will be further suppressed by the adverse policy climate regarding immigration and the potential addition of the citizenship question.
